# GAMA grass: interpreting sequence archives through multiple metadata lenses
**What *Tripsacum dactyloides* reveals about the value of integrated analysis workflows**

Eastern gamagrass (*Tripsacum dactyloides*) is a perennial crop wild relative of maize (*Zea mays*), associated with agronomically important characteristics such as apomixis and climate resilience (Kindiger *et al.*, 1996; Mammadov *et al.*, 2018). Beyond naming coincidence, the species serves as a compelling use-case because its archive structure highlights the value of GAMA’s full complement of functions. While each individual output shown below is informative, it is the context provided by their combination that produces the most coherent picture of data availability.

**Query provenance:** GAMA 0.3.2 | Query time (UTC): 2026-06-05T09:50:55Z | Databases: assembly, sra, biosample

## Lens 1: counting

*T. dactyloides* returned a data richness score of 15.8 (Tab. 1). For an underutilised plant, this indicates meaningful public data availability despite the apparent absence of a genome (Assembly = 0). Based on counts alone, secondary analyses using archived SRA records with associated BioSample context may be tentatively feasible, but reference-dependent workflows, including read mapping, variant calling, and comparative genomics, are less directly supported without a genome. Manual searching found that MaizeGDB (n.d.) lists two *T. dactyloides* assemblies from the Pan-Andropogoneae project (Td-FL_9056069_6-REFERENCE-PanAnd-2.0 and Td-KS_B6_1-REFERENCE-PanAnd-2.0), demonstrating a limitation of GAMA’s retrieval breadth rather than an absence of reference sequence resources. This first layer identifies a moderately well-researched species, while showing that data richness must be interpreted within the bounds of the archives it quantifies.

## Lens 2: classifying

Ontology-based classification revealed that the *T. dactyloides* archive profile is dominated by transcriptomic modality SRA records and ground anatomy BioSample records (Fig. 1). The disparity is clearest in SRA, where 180 of 192 records were classed as transcriptomic (93.8%), far exceeding the 10 genomic (5.2%) and 2 epigenomic (1.0%; Tab. 2). BioSample records show a similar pattern, with 139 of the 187 deemed operable classed as ground (74.3%), compared with 36 aerial (19.3%) and 6 reproductive (3.2%; Tab. 3). This additional layer refines the interpretation: *T. dactyloides* has a reasonable amount of data, but it is unevenly distributed across methodological and anatomical categories, thereby constraining the kinds of secondary analysis that can be immediately supported.

## Lens 3: connecting

The interaction layer connects SRA modality with BioSample anatomy and shows that the two dominant classification signals mainly coincide within linked BioSample records. The most abundant combination is transcriptomic × ground at 139 BioSample records (Tab. 4). However, its residual is small (0.323) because the observed count is close to the expected value of 135.243 generated by the large transcriptomic row and ground column totals. This is an important interpretive caution: residuals should not be read as *p*-values. Residuals indicate whether a cell is over- or under-represented relative to the distribution expected from the marginal profiles. The richest source of data for secondary analysis clearly derives from transcriptomic research on ground tissues, but the gaps are also informative. Targeted gene-expression studies of reproductive material could be a high-impact avenue for future sequencing efforts given its modest representation and the species’ relevance to our understanding of apomixis.

## Lens 4: contextualising

Replication skew shows that the dominant record classes are concentrated within very few BioProjects. The 180 transcriptomic SRA records are distributed among just 6 BioProjects, with the largest contributing 133 (73.9%; Tab. 5). BioSample records classed as ground are even more skewed, with 139 records distributed across 3 BioProjects, the largest once again contributing 133 (95.7%; Tab. 6). In ecological terms, this resembles a low-evenness distribution, where nominal BioProject richness exists but most records are concentrated in one dominant project. The inverse Simpson index (`eff`; effective number) reinforces this interpretation, reducing the transcriptomic signal to the equivalent of 1.743 evenly represented BioProjects and the ground signal to 1.091. For *T. dactyloides*, the transcriptomic-ground component remains useful for targeted reanalysis and hypothesis generation, but it is a weak foundation for meta-analysis-style synthesis.

## Taken together

These four analytical lenses transform a static archive count into a multi-dimensional profile of the *T. dactyloides* metadata landscape. An ostensibly promising data richness score is quickly qualified, classification exposes transcriptomic and ground-tissue dominance, interaction shows that the strongest signals converge in the same transcriptomic × ground combination, and skew reveals that this apparent abundance collapses into a handful of BioProjects – the final interpretation being that the low-evenness structure precludes robust meta-analysis, yet reanalysis within a niche evidential frame is still possible.

## Code

The tabular outputs presented in this use-case were produced with GAMA 0.3.2 (Lewis, 2026) using the script below (conducted 5 June 2026; 09:50:55 UTC). For a comprehensive overview of functions and methods, see the [GAMA user guide](../docs/GAMA_user_guide.md).

### Load package

```r
library(GAMA)
```

### Query NCBI databases for *Tripsacum dactyloides*

```r
RESULTS <- query_species('Tripsacum dactyloides')
```

### Summarise data richness

```r
SUMMARY <- summarise_availability(RESULTS)
print(SUMMARY)
```

### Summarise SRA modality composition

```r
SRA_SUMMARY <- summarise_sra_availability(RESULTS)
print(SRA_SUMMARY)
```

### Summarise BioSample anatomy composition

```r
BIO_SUMMARY <- summarise_biosample_availability(RESULTS)
print(BIO_SUMMARY)
```

### Summarise cross-database interaction

```r
INTERACTION <- summarise_interaction(SRA_SUMMARY, BIO_SUMMARY)
print(INTERACTION)
```

### Summarise SRA skew

```r
SRA_SKEW <- summarise_sra_skew(SRA_SUMMARY, class = 'transcriptomic')
print(SRA_SKEW)
```

### Summarise BioSample skew

```r
BIO_SKEW <- summarise_biosample_skew(BIO_SUMMARY, anatomy_class = 'ground')
print(BIO_SKEW)
```
